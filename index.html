Reference
Stoic Glossary
Plain-language definitions of terms used in Atarax
Philosophy & Practice
Stoicism
A practical philosophy founded in ancient Greece, teaching that we cannot control what happens to us — only how we respond. True wellbeing comes from living virtuously, not from wealth, pleasure, or status.
Virtue Ethics
The idea that living well means developing good character — being wise, brave, fair, and self-controlled — rather than chasing pleasure or following rigid rules. For Stoics, virtue is the only true good.
Dichotomy of Control
The core Stoic insight: some things are "up to us" (our thoughts, choices, and reactions) and some are not (everything else). Direct your energy only toward what you can control; release the rest.
Logos λόγος
The rational principle the Stoics believed governs the universe. Living "according to nature" means aligning your reason and actions with this universal order — not fighting reality, but flowing with it.
Eudaimonia εὐδαιμονία
Often translated as "happiness," but closer to "flourishing" or living a genuinely good life. For Stoics, eudaimonia is achieved through virtue alone — not luck, circumstances, or possessions.
Ataraxia ἀταραξία
A state of deep inner calm and freedom from mental disturbance. The name of this journal. Not the absence of emotion, but a steady, untroubled mind not swept away by fear, craving, or anxiety.
Apatheia ἀπάθεια
Not apathy, but freedom from destructive passions. Stoics still feel emotions — they simply don't let irrational impulses override clear judgment. Think: calm clarity, not coldness.
Key Practices
Premeditatio Malorum premeditation of evils
The practice of imagining in advance what could go wrong. By mentally rehearsing setbacks, you remove their power to shock you and train yourself to respond calmly rather than react with panic.
Memento Mori remember you will die
A practice of regularly reflecting on death — not morbidly, but to clarify what matters. Remembering life is finite strips away trivialities and focuses attention on how you actually spend your days.
Amor Fati love of fate
The practice of not merely accepting what happens, but embracing it wholeheartedly — including difficulty, failure, and loss. Turn every obstacle into fuel. Love your life exactly as it unfolds.
Negative Visualization
Deliberately imagining the loss of things you value — health, relationships, comfort — to cultivate genuine gratitude for what you already have. Closely related to Premeditatio Malorum.
The Four Virtues
Wisdom Sophia / Phronesis
The ability to see things clearly and make good judgments. Wisdom is knowing what is truly good, what is harmful, and how to act well in complex situations. It is the master virtue from which the others flow.
Courage Andreia
Not the absence of fear, but the willingness to act rightly despite it. Stoic courage includes enduring hardship, speaking truth, and persisting in virtue even when it is difficult or unpopular.
Justice Dikaiosyne
Treating others fairly, contributing to the common good, and fulfilling your duties to your community. For Stoics, we are all part of one human family — justice means acting accordingly.
Temperance Sophrosyne
Moderation, self-discipline, and balance. Neither extreme indulgence nor harsh deprivation — but choosing the right amount, at the right time, for the right reasons.
The Stoic Teachers
Marcus Aurelius 121–180 AD
Roman Emperor and Stoic philosopher. His private notebook — the Meditations — was never meant to be published. It records his daily effort to live by Stoic principles despite immense power and responsibility.
Epictetus c. 50–135 AD
Born a slave, he became one of Stoicism's greatest teachers. His core message: external circumstances don't define you — your choices do. His Discourses and Enchiridion remain profoundly practical.
Seneca c. 4 BC–65 AD
Roman statesman, playwright, and philosopher. His Letters to Lucilius are personal, warm, and accessible — 124 letters exploring how to live well, face death, and spend time wisely.
Other Terms
Hegemonikon ἡγεμονικόν
The "ruling faculty" — the rational, decision-making center of the mind. Stoics believed this is where your character lives and where all moral choices are made. Guarding it is your primary duty.
The Sage Sophos
The Stoic ideal of a perfectly wise and virtuous person. No one fully achieves this — but it serves as a north star. The goal is to make progress toward it each day, not to claim you've arrived.
Preferred Indifferents
Things like health, wealth, and good reputation are "indifferent" — they don't make you good or bad. But they are still "preferred" because they are better to have than not. The key: don't let them become your reason for living.
Morning & Evening Practice
Ancient Stoics began each day by setting intentions and anticipating challenges, and ended each day by reviewing their conduct honestly. This structure — used throughout Atarax — was central to their daily life.
